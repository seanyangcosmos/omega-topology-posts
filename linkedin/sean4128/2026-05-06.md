Treating evidence volume as evidence balance is a recurring failure mode in scientific interpretation. Dense mechanistic papers or well-framed pathways can feel decisive, yet topology reminds us that confidence is layer-dependent. Support that accumulates locally does not automatically transfer across the stack.

The constraint is simple: transfer is gated. Mechanistic alignment and genetic reinforcement may cohere, but without clinically expressed signal—and the human-layer work that makes that signal trustworthy—the system remains constrained. Ordering matters. Structure precedes interpretation; we do not read forward from enthusiasm, we read upward through closed layers.

Asymmetry is the norm, not the exception. A strong mechanistic story can coexist with weak or noisy clinical evidence; compelling genetics can lack dose–response clarity in people. When layers disagree, the decision layer arbitrates structure rather than averaging vibes. Uncertainty should be preserved where the stack is open, not collapsed for rhetorical comfort.

Decision takeaway: treat partial-stack convergence as directional, not dispositive. Define explicit transfer tests and thresholds per layer; make the promotion criteria visible before you promote the claim. Only when mechanistic, genetic, and clinical layers are closed—and the human layer has removed confound and design debt—should confidence propagate and interpretation be allowed to expand.
