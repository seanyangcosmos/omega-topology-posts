A common failure mode in reviews is to let the narrative lead the structure. A crisp overlay can feel like certainty, but clarity of explanation is not the same as closure of evidence. Topology asks us to construct the decision object first, then allow the narrative to translate it without softening constraints.

The constraint to protect is propagation order. Signals remain local until the next layer closes the gate. Mechanistic coherence does not become generalizable without genetic narrowing of causal space; clinical observations do not become policy without human‑layer readiness and operational fit. If a gate is open, confidence should stop there.

This is why asymmetry matters. You can have elegant mechanism and replicated preclinical patterning yet still be capped by equivocal human signal; or strong human correlation that cannot retro‑justify a missing mechanistic path. Averaging these conditions hides risk. Topology prevents incompatible uncertainties from being blended into a single score.

Operationally: 1) name the active gate and why it is open, 2) specify the minimum observation that would reopen transfer to the next layer, 3) distinguish supporting from advancing evidence in your plans, and 4) schedule decisions against these gates rather than dates. Interpretation follows structure; it never repairs it.
