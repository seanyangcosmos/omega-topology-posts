Reasoning failure mode: mistaking local coherence for global readiness.

A dataset can show strong internal agreement — clean mechanistic narratives, supportive genetics, even early clinical signals — and still be structurally constrained. Topology matters: confidence does not automatically propagate upward just because it is dense somewhere in the stack.

Constraint: transfer is a gated property, not a gradient. Alignment inside one layer changes local geometry but does not close gaps across layers. When the human layer (real-world use, behavior, incentives, operations) remains open, the system should treat advancement as provisional regardless of upstream strength.

Implication: uncertainty propagates asymmetrically. Missing or weak links in later layers dominate the decision surface, while excess strength upstream exhibits diminishing returns. The disciplined move is to invest effort where propagation breaks — interface definitions, measurement bridging, and feedback capture — rather than polishing already-coherent regions.

Decision posture: Separate “support accumulation” from “transfer eligibility.” Ask which specific gate blocks propagation, what evidence would close it, and what timeline or experiment can test that closure. This preserves stack integrity, avoids confidence leakage, and keeps interpretation downstream of structure.
