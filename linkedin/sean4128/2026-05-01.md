A reliable way to get lost in science is to let interpretation outrun structure. When the narrative leads, gaps get paved over with prose. Topology reverses that reflex: confidence moves only when a gate closes, and gates live at specific layers.

The order matters. Start in the core semantic layer by shaping evidence into comparable objects with clear boundaries. Move to the decision layer only when mechanistic, genetic, and clinical structures can be arbitrated against each other. Only then should the overlay translate that structure into narrative for humans. Skipping steps converts uncertainty into rhetoric.

Asymmetry is the default, not the exception. Mechanistic coherence can strengthen while genetics lags; genetics can hint ahead of clinical signal; a tidy clinical readout can still sit on a fuzzy pathway. Partial‑stack agreement looks persuasive but remains pre‑transfer until an explicit cross‑layer gate shuts.

Uncertainty propagates directionally. A mis‑specified mechanism contaminates genetic priors; weak genetic anchors cap clinical claims; clinical noise cannot repair upstream shape. The discipline is to name where uncertainty enters and to keep it local until corrected.

Decision implication: write the gating question into every plan. What must be true, at which layer, for confidence to advance? If the answer is hand‑wavy, you’re early. Keep building structure; let interpretation wait until the gate actually closes.
