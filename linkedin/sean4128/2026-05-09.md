A recurring mistake in scientific interpretation is treating evidence volume as evidence balance. Dense signals inside one stratum (mechanistic, genetic, or clinical) can look persuasive while the overall structure remains underdetermined. When the stack isn’t closed, adding more of the same kind of support increases local coherence but not transfer readiness.

Topology constraint: confidence is layer-dependent and transfer is a gated property. Ordering matters. Structure should be established before interpretation, and interpretation must not lead execution. In practice, this means anchoring to the core reasoning interface, arbitrating cross-evidence structure in the decision layer, then translating outcomes in the overlay layer. Reversing that order (starting with narrative and backfilling structure) is how uncertainty leaks forward.

Asymmetry shows up most clearly when two layers align and the third lags. Strong mechanistic plausibility plus genetic reinforcement does not guarantee near‑term clinical translation; equally, early clinical signal without mechanistic or genetic closure can be brittle and hard to generalize. Local support accumulates, but confidence does not automatically propagate across the stack.

Decision posture: preserve stack integrity. Ask which layer is actually constraining the decision, quantify what evidence would close that gap, and avoid narrative inflation while the gate is open. Progress is real when the constraining layer moves, not when the already‑strong layer gets denser. That shift—from counting signals to balancing structure—is the difference between momentum and maturity.
