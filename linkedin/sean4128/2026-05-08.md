A common trap in analysis is to mistake neat local closure for global readiness. When a pathway map looks clean or a dataset lines up internally, it tempts us to generalize. Topology treats that as a category error: layer-local certainty does not entitle cross-layer transfer.

The constraint is ordering. Evidence must climb through mechanistic plausibility, genetic reinforcement, and clinically expressed effect before interpretation is allowed to expand. Even then, the human layer can hold the system back—design debt, confound, operational variance, and incentives often reintroduce uncertainty that structure alone cannot cancel.

Asymmetry is informative, not embarrassing. Dense signal in one layer paired with noise in another is a structural fact about the world. Forcing symmetry by averaging or narrative smoothing only hides risk. The decision layer’s job is to preserve that asymmetry until specific transfer tests are passed.

Practice discipline: define promotion thresholds per layer before you look at results; write transfer tests that are falsifiable; quarantine interpretation debt until the human layer closes. When timelines compress, resist the urge to let coherence substitute for closure.

Decision takeaway: treat partial-stack convergence as directional evidence. Advance claims only when each upstream gate has closed and downstream uncertainty is explicitly bounded. Confidence should propagate because the structure allows it, not because the story wants it.
