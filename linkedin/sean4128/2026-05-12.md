A recurring failure mode in scientific interpretation is treating local coherence as global validation. When mechanistic and genetic signals line up, it is tempting to assume the stack is ready to advance. But topology forces us to ask whether confidence can legally propagate upward or outward, not just whether it accumulates within a single layer.

The constraint is order and gating. Omega’s discipline is simple: core reasoning first, decision arbitration second, overlay last. In practice, that means we evaluate cross-evidence structure before we allow narrative to form. Skipping this sequence—or blending layers—converts uncertainty into story rather than signal.

Evidence-layer asymmetry is the norm, not the exception. A strong mechanistic rationale with supportive genetics still cannot substitute for weak or noisy clinical signal, and human-layer closure remains a separate gate. Support can compound locally while transfer remains globally constrained. Partial-stack convergence changes interpretation geometry; it does not repair missing structure elsewhere.

Decision consequences follow. Do not compress layers into a single score. Preserve boundary integrity, define what is proven versus merely plausible, and make advancement contingent on the next missing bridge—often translational readiness or human-layer feedback. When that bridge is absent, the correct decision is restraint, not rhetorical elevation.

Working rule: topology before interpretation, structure before story, transfer as a gated property. If we hold that line, we reduce false advancement, surface the actual rate-limiting step, and keep scientific momentum aligned with what the stack can truly support today.
