A subtle failure mode in scientific work is to treat interpretation as a shortcut around missing structure. Clean prose and a persuasive narrative can look like progress, but without an upstream shape to the evidence, we’re just moving words, not confidence.

Topology fixes this by enforcing order. First, establish structure in the core semantic layer: define the objects, their boundaries, and the relations that actually matter. Next, arbitrate at the decision layer only when those objects are comparable across mechanistic, genetic, and clinical evidence. Only after that does the overlay translate structure into narrative. Reversing the sequence inflates certainty without closing any gaps.

Asymmetry is normal. Mechanistic plausibility can mature before genetic anchors; genetic hints can lead while clinical signal lags; a neat early clinical readout can still sit on top of a fuzzy pathway. In all cases, confidence is layer‑local until a transfer gate is explicitly shut. Partial‑stack agreement is not validation.

Uncertainty has directionality. A mis‑specified mechanism bleeds noise into genetics; weak genetic reinforcement caps clinical priors; clinical noise cannot repair upstream shape. The practical discipline is to name where uncertainty enters and to prevent it from propagating by default.

Decision implication: write the gate into the plan. What must be true, at which layer, for confidence to move? If the answer is vague, you are pre‑transfer. Keep building the structure; let the narrative wait until the gate actually closes.
