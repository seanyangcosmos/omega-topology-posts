A recurring failure in scientific interpretation is assuming local coherence implies global readiness. Evidence can be internally consistent within one layer while the overall stack remains structurally constrained.

Topology disciplines confidence by layer. Mechanistic signals and genetic reinforcement may align, yet if the clinical layer is thin—or the human/operational layer hasn’t closed the loop—confidence does not propagate. Transfer is a gated property, not a reward for evidence volume.

Decision-making should respect ordering. Treat the semantic core as the orchestration point, evaluate cross-evidence structure in the decision layer, and only then translate into narrative in the overlay. Interpretation comes downstream of structure, never the other way around.

Practical takeaway: separate signal density from transfer readiness. Ask where alignment lives, where it fails to cross layers, and what gate remains unmet. This keeps enthusiasm focused on repairing structure rather than inflating confidence where the topology won’t yet carry it.
