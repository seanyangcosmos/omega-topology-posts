A persistent failure mode in scientific decision‑making is letting coherence inside one evidence layer masquerade as system‑level certainty. Dense pathways, clean genetics, or tidy trial plots can each look decisive on their own. Omega topology treats these as local truths: valuable, but not yet transferable.

The constraint is structural: transfer is gated and ordered. Mechanistic plausibility and genetic reinforcement can align tightly while the clinical layer remains open, and even closed clinical signals can be held back when human‑layer issues (design debt, confound, operational noise) are unresolved. Structure precedes interpretation; we advance through layer closure, not enthusiasm.

Asymmetry is the default. Signals accumulate unevenly, and cross‑layer propagation should not be assumed. The decision layer arbitrates this—preserving uncertainty where the stack is open, rather than averaging confidence across incompatible evidence geometries. This keeps reasoning disciplined when timelines, endpoints, or populations diverge.

Decision takeaway: treat partial‑stack convergence as directional de‑risking, not destination. Make transfer tests explicit per layer, publish promotion thresholds in advance, and demand human‑layer closure before expanding claims. Confidence should propagate only when mechanistic, genetic, and clinical layers are closed and the interpretation risk is structurally contained.
