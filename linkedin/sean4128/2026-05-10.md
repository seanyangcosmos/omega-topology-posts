A recurring mistake in scientific interpretation is treating evidence volume as evidence balance. Dense signals inside one layer can feel decisive, yet the stack remains fragile if adjacent layers do not participate.

Topology separates local accumulation from cross-layer transfer. Mechanistic insight may cohere, but without genetic reinforcement and clinically expressed effect, confidence should not propagate. The order matters: establish structure first, then interpret—never the reverse.

Uncertainty also moves asymmetrically. Weakness at one layer can cap the entire system even when others are strong. The decision layer should arbitrate across structures, preserving the possibility that “more data” in one domain does not equal “more readiness” overall.

Good practice: state the constraint that blocks transfer, name the absent or incongruent layer, and defer narrative expansion until structure closes. Interpretation becomes safer—and strategy clearer—when we respect the gating between layers rather than smoothing over it.
