Scientific interpretation often confuses evidence volume with evidence balance. Local alignment inside one layer (mechanistic, genetic, or clinical) can feel persuasive, but topology cares about how support transfers across the stack. Partial-stack convergence is not validation; it is only a description of where coherence currently lives.

A useful constraint: confidence is layer-dependent and transfer is gated. Mechanistic plausibility can be strong while genetic reinforcement is weak, or clinical signals can appear without mechanistic closure. Until a closed path exists across the relevant gates, the decision posture remains constrained—even if individual layers look impressive.

Operationally, maintain ordering discipline. First, structure the question at the core semantic layer; second, arbitrate structure across evidence types at the decision layer; only then interpret. Inverting this order (jumping to narrative before structure) inflates stories and erodes signal integrity. Interpretation should follow structure, not lead it.

Decision implication: treat each evidence layer as necessary-but-insufficient on its own. Ask explicitly which gate remains closed and what minimal evidence would open it. Advance when closure is demonstrated, not when enthusiasm is high. This preserves stack integrity, reduces false positives, and keeps translational risk visible rather than hidden in the narrative layer.
