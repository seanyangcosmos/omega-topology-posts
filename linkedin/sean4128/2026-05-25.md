A recurring failure mode in scientific interpretation is treating evidence volume as evidence balance. More data inside one layer can tighten internal coherence without moving the system closer to a decision that transfers. Quantity is not symmetry, and density is not readiness.

Topology helps by separating local support from cross-layer transfer. Mechanistic plausibility, genetic reinforcement, and clinical signal maturity must each stand on their own terms before confidence can propagate. Support that accumulates in one stratum changes the geometry of interpretation there, but it does not repair missing structure elsewhere in the stack.

This is why partial‑stack convergence is inherently constrained. You can see elegant pathway logic and clean variant associations yet still face a translational gap when the human layer has not closed. Decision layers should preserve that gate: articulate what must be demonstrated for transfer, and resist collapsing uncertainty across boundaries just because one slice looks strong.

In practice: write hypotheses in layer order, specify closure conditions per layer, and define explicit transfer criteria (what evidence would allow confidence to move up or across). Let interpretation remain downstream of structure; summaries should explain which gates remain shut and why, not market around them.

No single layer carries the whole stack. Decisions improve when we honor that discipline—treating confidence as a routed, gated property rather than an average of impressions.
