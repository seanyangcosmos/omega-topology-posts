A recurring failure mode in scientific interpretation is treating evidence volume as evidence balance. More papers, larger datasets, or louder signals inside a single layer can look like progress, yet the decision geometry may be unchanged.

Topology imposes a constraint: confidence is layer-dependent and transfer is gated. Alignment within one layer (e.g., mechanistic or genetic) does not automatically propagate across the stack. The weakest unresolved layer sets the ceiling on advancement, regardless of how coherent the rest appears.

This creates asymmetry in uncertainty propagation. Mechanistic plausibility plus genetic reinforcement can still leave clinical maturity underdetermined; local certainty does not repair missing structure elsewhere. Decision arbitration should therefore evaluate cross-evidence coupling, not just intra-layer strength.

Operationally, preserve stack integrity. Sequence validation by layer, make coupling explicit, and require that evidence interacts across boundaries before interpreting momentum as readiness. Keep interpretation downstream of structure: narratives explain what the topology permits, not the other way around.

Advancement criteria should reflect this discipline: show where coupling closes, where it leaks, and what work would reduce tension at the boundary. When the stack remains partially converged, label it constrained rather than validated, and direct investment or research toward the true gating points.
