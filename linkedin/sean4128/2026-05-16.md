Many teams still mistake evidence quantity for balance. Dense signal inside one evidence layer can reshape local interpretation, but it does not automatically travel across the stack. Transfer is gated. Until a downstream layer closes, upstream coherence remains a local property rather than a global verdict.

A useful constraint is to respect propagation order. Mechanistic plausibility can invite confidence only when genetic reinforcement narrows causal ambiguity, and clinical signal then demonstrates effect in the intended human context. If a lower layer is open, the upper layers should be read as directional guidance, not as validation. Confidence accumulates conditionally, not absolutely.

Decision arbitration should explicitly encode this asymmetry. A portfolio or program facing strong mechanistic and genetic alignment but sparse or noisy clinical data is not “validated”; it is structurally constrained. The correct move is disciplined exploration: specify what clinical observations would discharge the remaining uncertainty, and schedule decisions against those gates.

Uncertainty also flows unevenly. Weakness in a lower layer can cap the whole structure, while strength in an upper layer should not be allowed to retro-justify gaps beneath it. Keep interpretation downstream of structure: first build the decision object from the evidence layers, then translate it for humans without relaxing the original constraints.

A short checklist for upcoming reviews: 1) name the active topology constraint (where propagation currently stops), 2) state the minimum evidence needed to reopen transfer to the next layer, 3) distinguish “supporting” from “advancing” evidence, and 4) avoid language that collapses layer boundaries. Treat call order as policy: reason on structure first, arbitrate decisions second, convert to narrative last.
