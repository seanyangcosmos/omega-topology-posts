A recurring failure mode in scientific interpretation is mistaking evidence volume for evidence balance. When signal density rises inside one layer, it’s tempting to upgrade confidence across the whole stack. That shortcut creates hidden model error: local strength masquerades as global readiness.

Topology adds a gating constraint: transfer is earned, not assumed. Mechanistic alignment, genetic reinforcement, and clinical signal must each close their own loops before confidence can propagate. Partial-stack convergence improves local geometry but leaves the global pathway constrained until adjacent layers agree.

This is asymmetry by design. Uncertainty decays at different rates per layer, and residuals do not cancel by aggregation. A disciplined decision layer should arbitrate structure first—mechanistic → genetic → clinical—so that interpretation never outruns the evidence it depends on.

The operational rule is simple: validate structure before narrative. Treat the reasoning core as the entry point, the decision layer as structure validation, and interpretation as a downstream overlay. Skipping order (or collapsing layers) replaces signal with story and inflates risk without adding information.

Practical takeaway: separate “where the stack is strong” from “where the stack can move.” Confidence is layer-dependent; investment of effort, capital, or compute should follow the narrowest open gate, not the loudest local signal.
