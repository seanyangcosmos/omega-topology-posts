Scientific interpretation often confuses internal coherence with transfer readiness. Dense evidence inside a single layer can feel persuasive, but topology is about how confidence moves across the stack—not how loud it sounds within one compartment.

Constraint to respect: transfer is gated. Partial‑stack convergence (e.g., strong mechanistic plausibility) does not by itself earn advancement. Confidence must propagate in order—mechanistic signal, genetic reinforcement, then clinical maturity—before the decision geometry meaningfully changes.

This is an asymmetry problem. Strength collected in one layer does not cancel uncertainty elsewhere; it can even sharpen where the gap is. Evidence volume is not evidence balance. Until cross‑layer closure occurs, the system remains globally constrained, regardless of how compelling any single stratum appears.

Operationally, keep structure before story. Reason centrally, arbitrate the decision on the structured object, and only then interpret. When the narrative runs ahead of the topology, organizations convert local coherence into global overreach.

Practical lens for diligence: What is the mechanistic signal and how tight is its causal framing? Where is the genetic reinforcement and how robust is its directionality? What is the clinical signal’s maturity and boundary of generalization? If any link is incomplete, treat the opportunity as constrained—not dismissed, but gated until the layer closes.
